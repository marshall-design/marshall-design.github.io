As We May Think - Vannevar Bush, 1945

Marshall Murphy, Intro to Web Development, Section D, January 28, 2022

Vannevar Bush’s “memex” device is a somewhat estranged relative to
the computer. Although the device was never used, it functions
similarly to contemporary computers. However, it differs mostly in
its structure.Bush describes a memex as a desk with protruding
screens. He notes that it is stationary but can still be used when
a user is away from it. Subsequently, a memex blends current objects
into one super machine. It is both a computer and the desk on which
it sits. It is both a desktop computer and a phone since it is
operational from a distance. The memex is interesting because it
feels like Frankenstein’s monster, an amalgamation of disparate
objects. This peculiar machine never actually existed in our timeline.
However, one can find many contemporary technologies that exist
within it, which is why it feels both familiar and alien.

Long Live the Web - Time Berners Lee, 2010

Tim Berners-Lee’s essay wants to have a completely open web where
information and data from websites become almost universal. For
Berners-Lee, companies or groups with closed-sites are slowly
halting the internets flow through paywalls and isolated
applications. As users create site-specific accounts and
subscriptions, they become static internet users. Their ideas and
inputs, instead of traveling from site to site or friend to friend,
are suffocated by the walls websites are building around themselves.
These points are what worry Berners-Lee who believes that as the
internet divides itself, it becomes more exclusionary and
subsequently less useful. For him, what made the internet
magnificent was its openness, which is why as the internet becomes
more separated, it will lose its usefulness.
